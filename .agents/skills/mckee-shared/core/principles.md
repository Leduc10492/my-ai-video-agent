# Principles (McKee)

## Core principles

- Originality is the fusion of content and form; novelty for its own sake becomes empty.
- A story is both what you tell and how you tell it; stale content leads to stale form.
- Structure is the strategic selection and ordering of events to create meaning and emotion.
- Events must occur within scenes, and scenes must be driven by conflict to generate change.

## Values and conflict

- Story meaning is expressed through value change (positive to negative or the reverse).
- A strong event creates a value reversal rather than a flat repetition.
- Avoid coincidence as a major cause of change; it weakens causality and meaning.

## Scene

- A scene is a continuous time-space block in which conflict produces a value change.
- Scene check: identify the key value at the start and at the end; if no change, the scene is likely exposition.
- A turning point is required: the moment that flips or intensifies the value state.

## Sequence

- A sequence is 2–5 scenes with rising impact that culminate in a peak.
- Each scene should intensify the value conflict toward the sequence climax.

## Structure

- Structure is not formula; it is the disciplined choice of events that embody the story's values and meaning.
- A well-designed structure integrates theme, character, and conflict into a coherent progression.
- A story spine has five parts: inciting incident, progressive complications, crisis, climax, resolution.
- The inciting incident must be a dynamic event that breaks equilibrium and flips a value.
- The inciting incident should happen early (roughly the first quarter of the story).
- The inciting incident projects a mandatory crisis scene; the audience expects to see it.
- Keep a causal lock: because of the inciting incident, the climax must occur.

## Character and desire

- The protagonist must possess a conscious desire (goal) and the will to pursue it.
- Without clear desire or decisive action, the story cannot move or transform.
- Strong stories reveal the gap between a character's surface and inner nature; the arc is the revealed change.
- A true choice carries a cost; gaining one value often means losing another.
- Strong antagonistic forces (inner, personal, social, environmental) should combine to pressure the protagonist into decisive action.

## Theme

- Theme is argued through repeated value conflicts and their outcomes.
- The climax resolves the central value conflict and declares the story's final meaning.

## Pacing and climax

- A climax is a profound value change, not just a loud moment.
- Alternate tension and relief to create rising waves toward higher peaks.
- As the climax approaches, shorten or tighten scenes to accelerate pace.

## Exposition and backstory

- Exposition should be dramatized; reveal only when the audience needs and wants it.
- Backstory reveals can create turning points when placed at moments of crisis.
- Flashbacks should be mini-stories with their own inciting incident and turning point.

## Audience-knowledge modes

- Mystery: audience knows less than the character; curiosity about facts drives interest.
- Suspense: audience knows the same; curiosity about outcome drives interest.
- Dramatic irony: audience knows more; interest shifts to how and why characters act.

## Common mistakes

- Mistaking novelty for originality.
- Expository scenes with no value change.
- Coincidental events doing the work of conflict.
- A passive protagonist who wants nothing and decides nothing.
- An inciting incident that is off-screen or too late.
- Breaking the causal link between inciting incident and climax.
